<!-- _class: part1 -->

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d$ denote a $d$-dimensional comnputational domain and let $u: \Omega \to \R$ represent an unknown scalar field defined over this domain.

This scalar field is assumed to represent a solution to the scalar partial differential equation:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \forall \x \in \Omega, \quad \L[u](\x) = s(\x), \quad \text{e.g.} \ \L[u] = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \dfrac{\partial f(u)}{\partial x}\\
        & \forall \x \in \partial \Omega, \quad \B[u](\x) = g(\x), \quad \text{e.g.} \ \B[u] = u, \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}, \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

In a classical numerical method, one is typically trying to approximate an infinite-dimensional function space defined over the computational domain $\Omega$ by a finite one $\V_h = \set{\varphi_1, \cdots, \varphi_N}$.

---

<!-- _class: part1 -->

$\varphi_i: \Omega \to \R$ is a scalar-valued basis function, such that our PDE solution $u$ is approximated by the linear combination $u_h(\x) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^n U_i \varphi_i(\x)$, $(u_h: \Omega \to \R) \in \V_h$, $N$ degrees of freedom, arbitrary $U_i \in \R$.

Consider the case of a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) consisting of a single hidden layer, itself comprising $N$ hidden neurons.

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \forall i \in \set{1, \cdots, N}, \quad \psi_i \left(\x; W^{(1)}, \b^{(1)} \right) = \sigma \left(\sum\limits_{j=1}^d W_{ij}^{(1)} \x_j + \b_j^{(1)} \right) \\
        & u_\th(\x) = \b^{(2)} + \sum\limits_{i=1}^N W_{1i}^{(2)} \psi_i \left(\x; W^{(1)}, \b^{(1)} \right), W^{(1)} \in \R^{N \times d},  W^{(2)} \in \R^{1 \times N}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

Total trainable parameters $P = Nd + N + N + 1 = 1 + (d + 2)N$.

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

Suppose that we have a first-order hyperbolic PDE in conservation law form, evolving in one spatial dimension, with no source terms:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \dfrac{\partial f(u)}{\partial x} = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + f^\prime(u) \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 0 \\
        & u(0, x) = u_0(x)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

Use the method of characteristics

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\d u}{\d t} = 0, \quad \text{along curves satisfying} \quad \dfrac{\d x}{\d t} = f^\prime(u)
\end{equation}
$$

Integrate by time

$$
\begin{equation}
    x - x_0 = f^\prime(u) (t - t_0) = f^\prime(u_0(x_0))t
\end{equation}
$$

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

Thus, $x = x_0 + f^\prime(u_0(x_0))t$ is the equation for a straight characteristic line emanating from point $x_0$, and we have

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial x_0} = 1 + f^{\prime\prime}(u_0(x_0)) u_0^\prime(x_0)t
\end{equation}
$$

Again using the  fact that $u(x, t) = u_0(x_0)$ for all $t \ge 0$ along characteristic lines, we can evaluate this derivative via the chain rule as:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \dfrac{\partial u_0(x_0)}{\partial x} = \dfrac{u_0^\prime (x_0)}{1 + f^{\prime\prime}(u_0(x_0)) u_0^\prime(x_0)t}
\end{equation}
$$

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

<div class="block theorem">
<div class="label"> Lemma 1 </div>

Suppose that $u: \Omega \to \R$ is a solution to a (homogeneous) first-order hyperbolic PDE in conservation law form:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \dfrac{\partial f(u)}{\partial x} = 0
\end{equation}
$$

where the initial data: $u(0, x) = u_0(x)$ is given by a $C^n$-smooth function $u_0: \R \to \R$, and the flux is given by a $C^{n+1}$-smooth function $f: \R \to \R$, where $n \ge 1$. Then:

</div>

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

<div class="block theorem">
<div class="label"> lemma 1 </div>

If the flux is linear (i.e. $f^{\prime\prime} = 0$), or the flux is convex and the initial data is monotonically increasing (i.e. $f^{\prime\prime} \ge 0$ and $u_0^\prime \ge 0$), or the flux is concave and the initial data is monotonically decreasing (i.e. $f^{\prime\prime} \le 0$ and $u_0^\prime \le 0$), then the solution $u$ remains $C^n$-smooth for all $t \ge 0$.

</div>

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

<div class="block theorem">
<div class="label"> lemma 1 </div>

Otherwise, the solution $u$ remains $C^n$-smooth for all $0 \le t < t_\infty$, where:

$$
\begin{equation}
    t_\infty = \left(\sup\limits_{x_0 \in \R} \set{-f^{\prime\prime}(u_0(x_0)) u_0^\prime(x_0)} \right)^{-1}
\end{equation}
$$

and is discontinuous (though still piecewise $C^n$) for all $t \ge t_\infty$.

</div>

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

<div class="block theorem">
<div class="label"> theorem 2 </div>

Suppose that (... same as **Lemma 1**), morover, that

$$
\begin{equation}
    \V_{NN} = \set{u_\th \mid \th \in \R^P}
\end{equation}
$$

represents the space of possible functions $u_\th: \Omega \to \R$ that can be computed by a MLP with $P$ trainable parameters, comprising a single hidden layer consisting of $N$ hidden neurons, with the activation function given by a $C^\infty$-smooth function $\sigma: \R \to \R$ that is not a polynomial on any subinterval of $\R$. Then:

</div>

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

<div class="block theorem">
<div class="label"> theorem 2 </div>

If (... same as **Lemma 1**) then there exists a neural network $u_\th \in \V_{NN}$ such that:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \forall t \ge 0, \quad \lVert u - u_\th \rVert_\infty = \O \left(N^{-\frac{n}{d}} \right)
\end{equation}
$$

Otherwise, there exists a neural network $u_\th \in \V_{NN}$ such that:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \forall 0 \le t < t_\infty, \quad \lVert u - u_\th \rVert_\infty = \O \left(N^{-\frac{n}{d}} \right) \\
        & \forall t \ge t_\infty, \quad \lVert u - u_\th \rVert_\infty = \O(1)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

</div>

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

<div class="block theorem">
<div class="label"> theorem 2 </div>

In this case, there nevertheless exists a function:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{u}(t, x) =
    \begin{cases}
        u_{\th_1}^{(1)}, \quad x \le x_1(t) \\
        u_{\th_2}^{(2)}, \quad x_1(t) < x \le x_2(t) \\
        \cdots
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
$$

where each $u_{\th_i}^{(i)} \in \V_{NN}$ is a neural network, and each $x_i: \R \to \R$ is a function tracking the position of a single  discontinuity, such that:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \forall t \ge 0, \quad \lVert u - \widetilde{u} \rVert_\infty = \O \left(N^{-\frac{n}{d}} \right)
\end{equation}
$$

</div>

---

<!-- _class: part3 -->

Assemble the solution as a composition: $u(t, x) = f(g(t, x), h(t, x))$.

If the functions $f, g$ and $h$ are $C^\alpha$-, $C^\beta$-, and $C^\gamma$-smooth, respectively, then their composition $f(g(t, x), h(t, x))$ is $C^{\min\set{\alpha, \beta, \gamma}}$-smooth. Thus, at least one of the three functions in the composition must be at least as discontinuous as the function $u(t, x)$ itself.

Let $f, g$ be arbitray functions and $\tilde{f}, \tilde{g}$ be shallow neural network with $L^\infty$ errors of $e_f$ and $e_g$.

$$
\begin{equation}
    \lVert f \circ g - \tilde{f} \circ \tilde{g}\rVert_\infty \le \lVert f \circ g - f \circ \tilde{g}\rVert_\infty + \lVert f \circ \tilde{g} - \tilde{f} \circ \tilde{g}\rVert_\infty
\end{equation}
$$

---

<!-- _class: part3 -->

Suppose $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz everywhere, then the composition of (shallow) neural network approximations is a (deep) neural network approximation with $L^\infty$ error bounded by:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \lVert f \circ g - \tilde{f} \circ \tilde{g}\rVert_\infty \le e_f + L e_g
\end{equation}
$$

For example, a good general candidate is

$$
\begin{equation}
    f(x) = \dfrac{\sinh^{-1}(x)}{C}, \quad g(x) = \sinh(Cu(x))
\end{equation}
$$
