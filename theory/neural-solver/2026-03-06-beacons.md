<!-- _class: part1 -->

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d$ denote a $d$-dimensional comnputational domain and let $u: \Omega \to \R$ represent an unknown scalar field defined over this domain.

This scalar field is assumed to represent a solution to the scalar partial differential equation:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \forall \x \in \Omega, \quad \L[u](\x) = s(\x), \quad \text{e.g.} \ \L[u] = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \dfrac{\partial f(u)}{\partial x}\\
        & \forall \x \in \partial \Omega, \quad \B[u](\x) = g(\x), \quad \text{e.g.} \ \B[u] = u, \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}, \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

In a classical numerical method, one is typically trying to approximate an infinite-dimensional function space defined over the computational domain $\Omega$ by a finite one $\V_h = \set{\varphi_1, \cdots, \varphi_N}$.

---

<!-- _class: part1 -->

$\varphi_i: \Omega \to \R$ is a scalar-valued basis function, such that our PDE solution $u$ is approximated by the linear combination $u_h(\x) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^n U_i \varphi_i(\x)$, $(u_h: \Omega \to \R) \in \V_h$, $N$ degrees of freedom, arbitrary $U_i \in \R$.

Consider the case of a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) consisting of a single hidden layer, itself comprising $N$ hidden neurons.

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \forall i \in \set{1, \cdots, N}, \quad \psi_i \left(\x; W^{(1)}, \b^{(1)} \right) = \sigma \left(\sum\limits_{j=1}^d W_{ij}^{(1)} \x_j + \b_j^{(1)} \right) \\
        & u_\th(\x) = \b^{(2)} + \sum\limits_{i=1}^N W_{1i}^{(2)} \psi_i \left(\x; W^{(1)}, \b^{(1)} \right), W^{(1)} \in \R^{N \times d},  W^{(2)} \in \R^{1 \times N}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

Total trainable parameters $P = Nd + N + N + 1 = 1 + (d + 2)N$.

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

Suppose that we have a first-order hyperbolic PDE in conservation law form, evolving in one spatial dimension, with no source terms:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        & \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \dfrac{\partial f(u)}{\partial x} = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} + f^\prime(u) \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 0 \\
        & u(0, x) = u_0(x)
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

Use the method of characteristics

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\d u}{\d t} = 0, \quad \text{along curves satisfying} \quad \dfrac{\d x}{\d t} = f^\prime(u)
\end{equation}
$$

Integrate by time

$$
\begin{equation}
    x - x_0 = f^\prime(u) (t - t_0) = f^\prime(u_0(x_0))t
\end{equation}
$$

---

<!-- _class: part2 -->

Thus, $x = x_0 + f^\prime(u_0(x_0))t$ is the equation for a straight characteristic line emanating from point $x_0$, and we have

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial x_0} = 1 + f^{\prime\prime}(u_0(x_0)) u_0^\prime(x_0)t
\end{equation}
$$

Again using the  fact that $u(x, t) = u_0(x_0)$ for all $t \ge 0$ along characteristic lines, we can evaluate this derivative via the chain rule as:

$$
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \dfrac{\partial u_0(x_0)}{\partial x} = \dfrac{u_0^\prime (x_0)}{1 + f^{\prime\prime}(u_0(x_0)) u_0^\prime(x_0)t}
\end{equation}
$$
